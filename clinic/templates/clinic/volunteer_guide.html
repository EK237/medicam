Introduction

Thank you and welcome to doc19.org, a website that was created to help connect those who need help during the COVID-19 crisis with volunteers who are willing to listen and give a hand. Your help is much appreciated. This guide was created to help orient you to your role as a volunteer. It's still a work in progress, so please let us know if you have feedback.

Please familiarize yourself with the entire guide, including associated information and resources, to be better prepared to answer questions.

Volunteers may provide the following services:

  • Triage
  • Emotional support
  • Referral to official resources

For liability reasons, we ask that you do not to give individual medical advice. Ensure that callers are aware of this before you proceed.

We recommend the following resources:

  • US: CDC Coronavirus Self-Checker
  • Canada: Government of Canada Coronavirus website
  • UK: NHS Coronavirus advice

Everything you say is your responsibility. We cannot be held responsible for information provided to callers.

How you approach calls is left up to your judgement. In the next section, we'll set out some suggestions for introducing yourself.

How to introduce yourself

You can say:

Hi there, I'm [name]. I'm here to help you today as a volunteer. [Before we continue, I would like to let you know that I am not a licensed medical professional.] I can provide you with information in order to help you make a well-informed decision. However, I can't offer medical advice.

Or:

Hello, my name is [name] and I am a volunteer. [Before we proceed, I would like to let you know that I'm not yet a licensed medical professional.] I'm here to provide you with information in order to help you make a well-informed decision; but please be aware that I can't offer medical advice.

If the caller agrees to proceed:

Which country do you currently live in?

  • If US/Canada, follow respective websites and resources.
  • If from elsewhere, follow CDC guidance but be aware that self-checker is intended for people currently located in the US.

Follow up with:

How can I help you today?

If you are asked about...

Symptoms

I'm going to walk you through the tool provided by [public health authority] for the Coronavirus. Just note that this tool is not intended for diagnosis or treatment of disease, including COVID-19, as that judgment must be left up to your healthcare provider. Can we begin?

If yes: ask the questions from the tool. If no: give them the link to the tool.

Emotional support

Your role is best served by identifying the problem that the caller has, not to offer personal advice on solving the problem.

If a caller is suicidal, keep them online, and have them call the National Suicide Hotline: 1-800-273-8255 (US)

What you should do:

  • Make sure you compliment them (You're so strong for talking about this.)
  • Make sure you empathize (I can see how that would make you feel, Iâ€™m sorry that that happened)
  • Listen!
If you can identify a problem that you think can be addressed with a resource, then offer the resource.

Information about COVID-19

Familiarize yourself with our frequently asked questions page. If you don't know the answer to an question, please direct the caller to one of the official resources. You can also help them search for the information they need.

COVID-19 is a highly contagious viral infection that transmits airborne, through sneezing, coughing, or contact with shared surfaces. People who develop an infection typically do after 2-14 days after contact with an infected individual. Common symptoms are fever, cough, or shortness of breath.

Right now, it's important not to travel unless absolutely necessary. When going out, practice social distancing and stand at least 6 feet away from people. If someone is sick and must go outside, they should wear a face mask.

How do I protect myself?
  1. Wash your hands often
  2. Don't touch your face and mouth
  3. Avoid close contact with people
How do I protect others?
  1. Stay home if you're sick, unless you need medical care
  2. Cover your coughs and sneezes
  3. When you're sick, wear a mask if you must be around people
  4. Clean and disinfect commonly shared surfaces

For more information about self-protection and protecting others, these resources may be helpful:

  • US: https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/prevent-getting-sick/prevention.html
  • Canada: https://www.canada.ca/en/public-health/services/diseases/2019-novel-coronavirus-infection/prevention-risks.html

Further resources

(US) The National Alliance on Mental Illness produced a Resource Guide for those seeking help. Topics include:

  1. Mental, social support
  2. Problems with getting care due to insurance problems
  3. Homelessness
  4. Other problems

Familiarize yourself with this manual; there are great resources in there that you can direct callers to. If necessary, you can also refer them to 211.org or 211.ca.

211 always has someone available 24/7 who can help callers find what they need. This is the place to get help finding shelter or food, or for help dealing with domestic violence.
